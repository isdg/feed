# Using DNSSEC

I was on StackOverflow the other day when I saw [a nifty post](https://unix.stackexchange.com/questions/90035/how-to-set-dns-resolver-in-fedora-using-network-manager) on using custom DNS servers with [NetworkManager](https://wiki.gnome.org/Projects/NetworkManager). As you can see, in the middle of the post there's a link to [https://dnssec.vs.uni-due.de](https://dnssec.vs.uni-due.de). Being a curious sort, I opened it up and saw that it's a DNSSEC tester!

[DNSSEC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Domain_Name_System_Security_Extensions) is a protocol for ensuring that [DNS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Domain_Name_System) results are accurate, using [public-key cryptographpy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Public-key_cryptography). It prevents [cache poisoning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DNS_spoofing) and [dns hijacking](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DNS_hijacking). It also prevents [court-ordered DNS bans](https://blog.xs4all.nl/xs4all-moet-adressen-pirate-bay-voorlopig-blokkeren/) and [hidden redirects](https://blog.wired.com/27bstroke6/2008/04/isps-error-page.html), both of which violate [RFC](https://www.ietf.org/rfc.html) standards.

Unfortunately, most computers in the world do *not* use DNSSEC by default. Most end-users use DNS servers provided by their [ISP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Internet_service_provider), which often don't support DNSSEC at all. Fortunately, configuring it is a simple process for which Google has an [extensive walkthrough](https://developers.google.com/speed/public-dns/docs/using), as well as [an overview](https://developers.google.com/speed/public-dns/) of DNS in general.
